Turning sales data into direction

A multi-location retailer used product, regional and fulfillment analytics to move from reactive reporting to clearer decisions about inventory, marketing and operational cost.

The challenge

Years of order and sales data existed, but leadership could not clearly see which choices were driving performance.

A growing retailer had data, but not enough direction

A multi-location retailer doing over $5 million in annual sales had years of order and sales data spread across spreadsheets, but no clear view of what was actually driving performance. Leadership could see topline revenue, but could not answer the questions that mattered most.

  • Which product categories were genuinely growing, and which were quietly declining?
  • Was the business paying for fulfillment and shipping inefficiently?
  • Where were the next real opportunities for growth by product and by region?

This is the classic bind for a business at this scale: too big to run on gut feel, too lean to justify a full-time in-house analyst, and too small a budget to bring in a large consultancy. The owner needed genuine analytical rigor, sized and priced for a small business.

Without that visibility, decisions on inventory, marketing spend and fulfillment were being made on instinct rather than evidence.

The approach

We built one analytics view across product performance and order/payment operations.

Sales trend monitoring

We consolidated over 1,500 rows of sales data covering 31,000+ items sold and nearly 700 unique products, then broke performance down year-over-year by category, product and location.

Decline detection

Rather than relying on total revenue alone, we tracked category-level and location-level splits across operating regions to catch declines masked by strong performers elsewhere.

Order and fulfillment analysis

We broke down payment status, fulfillment status and shipping cost trends order-by-order, isolating where cost was leaking and where paid orders were stuck unfulfilled or partially fulfilled.

Opportunity mapping

Combining both views, we identified where demand was shifting geographically and which underweighted categories represented the clearest next bets for inventory and marketing investment.

What we found

The useful story was hiding below the topline

01

Concentrated growth

A small number of categories accounted for a disproportionate share of sales growth, while several legacy categories dragged the average down.

02

Fulfillment cost leakage

A growing share of paid orders were sitting unfulfilled or partially fulfilled, tying up cost without corresponding revenue.

03

Regional divergence

Performance by region told a different story than the national total, showing where marketing and stock should be reallocated.

04

Emerging categories

Fast-growing but still-small categories were easy to miss in a topline view, but represented outsized future opportunity.

The outcome

From reactive reports to proactive decisions

Armed with a clear, ongoing view of category trends, fulfillment cost and regional performance, the retailer reallocated inventory and marketing spend toward the categories and regions the data showed were working, and tightened fulfillment operations where cost was quietly building up.

10% Operating costs dropped, driven largely by tighter fulfillment and shipping efficiency.
20% Sales grew over the following six months, led by the strongest identified product and regional opportunities.

Why it worked

The value was not one dashboard. It was a repeatable way to see the business.

One joined-up view gave leadership confidence to act

The turnaround was not a single dashboard. It was a repeatable way of seeing the business: sales trends, category-level declines and payment/fulfillment health, all in one place, refreshed on real order data rather than assumptions.

Just as importantly, it was delivered at a scale that made sense for the business. The owner got the clarity a larger consultancy or in-house analytics team would provide, without the overhead of a full-time salary or a large retainer.
